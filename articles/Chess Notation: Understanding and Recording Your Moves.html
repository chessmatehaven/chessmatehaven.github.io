Algebraic Notation: The Modern Standard

Algebraic notation is the most widely used system to record chess moves today. It is easy to understand, concise, and universally recognized. In algebraic notation, each square on the chessboard is represented by a unique combination of a letter and a number.

The chessboard consists of 64 squares, arranged in an 8x8 grid. The files, or columns, are labeled from 'a' to 'h,' starting from the left side of the board. The ranks, or rows, are numbered from 1 to 8, starting from the bottom. This alphanumeric system allows us to identify each square unambiguously.

To record a move, we use the following format: [Piece][Destination Square]. For example, if a pawn moves to e5, we would write 'e5.' If a knight captures a piece on d7, we would record the move as 'Nxd7.' It's important to note that when a pawn makes a capture, we include the file of its starting position, e.g., 'exd5.' Additionally, we can use '+' to indicate check and '#' to indicate checkmate.

Recording Moves and Captures

Recording moves and captures using algebraic notation is relatively straightforward once you understand the basics. Let's look at some examples to illustrate the process:

  • Pawn Moves: A pawn move is recorded by specifying the destination square. For instance, if a pawn moves to e4, we write 'e4.'
  • Pawn Captures: When a pawn captures a piece, we include the file of its starting position, followed by an 'x,' and then the destination square. For example, if a pawn on d4 captures a piece on e5, we record the move as 'dxe5.'
  • Other Pieces: Moves made by pieces other than pawns are indicated by the uppercase first letter of the piece's name, followed by the destination square. For instance, if a knight moves to f3, we write 'Nf3.'
  • Piece Captures: Piece captures are denoted by the capturing piece's letter, an 'x,' and the destination square. For example, if a bishop captures a piece on e6, we record the move as 'Bxe6.'

Remember to annotate your moves with '+' for check and '#' for checkmate when applicable. These annotations provide additional information about the move and are particularly useful for analysis purposes.

Common Notational Symbols and Abbreviations

In addition to the basic algebraic notation, there are some common symbols and abbreviations used in chess notation to provide further clarity:

  • Castling: Kingside castling is represented as '0-0,' and queenside castling is represented as '0-0-0.'
  • En Passant: En passant captures are denoted by the capturing pawn's file, an 'x,' and the destination square, followed by 'e.p.' For example, if a black pawn on d4 captures an en passant on e3, we write 'dxe3e.p.'
  • Promotion: Pawn promotion is indicated by specifying the destination square and the promoted piece, separated by an equals sign ('=') or a hyphen ('-'). For example, if a pawn on a7 promotes to a queen on a8, we record the move as 'a8=Q.'
